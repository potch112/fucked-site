Somewhere between a campaign launch and a class war, Chris Hipkins has revived the capital gains tax—New Zealand’s favourite policy ghost, doomed to haunt every election until the heat death of the sun. He says it’s about fairness and funding GP visits; Christopher Luxon says it’s about punishing success, which in local dialect translates to “please don’t tax my beach house portfolio.” Then, because the gods demand entertainment, Hipkins pointed out that Luxon made more flipping houses than running the country. It’s fucked, brilliantly fucked, in that peculiarly Kiwi way where everyone pretends to be middle class while owning three investment properties and a bach.

By mid-speech, Hipkins was promising free doctors, fair pay, and something called a “Medicard,” which sounds like a loyalty scheme for the under-funded. Luxon, meanwhile, was probably checking realestate.co.nz for another quick flip. The Greens hovered on the sidelines like eager tax interns, promising to go “further,” which usually means “make everyone hate us a little bit more.”

So here we are again: one party taxing the rich, the other defending the rich, both surrounded by rich MPs in suits worth more than a GP’s yearly ACC claim. It’s fucked because it’s familiar, fucked because it’s predictable, and fucked because nobody will actually pass the tax anyway. In the end, New Zealand will do what it always does—argue, stall, and quietly hope property prices keep going up. Nothing ever truly unfucks here; it just appreciates in value.
