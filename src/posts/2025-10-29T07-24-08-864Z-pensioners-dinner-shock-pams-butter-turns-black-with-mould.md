Ah, the humble block of butter. A staple, a comfort, a silent promise of smooth, golden indulgence. Margaret Brain, 73, from Hastings, approached her dinner with quiet anticipation. But alas, the universe had other plans. Instead of familiar, creamy yellow, she was greeted by a culinary anathema: a 500g block, blackened with mould at its very core. One must appreciate the exquisite irony of a product, packed on April 7th and deemed 'best before' April 7th *next year*, achieving such decomposition with remarkable haste. It’s impressive how thoroughly fucked the quality control must have been.

"You expect mould on cheese maybe but not on butter," she rightly observed, articulating the fundamental tenets of dairy expectation. Yet, when confronted with this domestic horror, the official response from Foodstuffs is, predictably, a masterclass in corporate deflection. "Alert a team member immediately for proper investigation," they intone, as if Mrs. Brain should have paused mid-gag to conduct a forensic examination. The sheer disconnect is breathtaking. One pictures a beleaguered team member, clipboard in hand, arriving at Mrs. Brain’s kitchen, ready to document the fungal insurrection. Meanwhile, Mrs. Brain simply wants her $8.99 back.

The whispers on the online community group suggest a possible culprit: a "higher water content." Ah, the relentless march of progress! Less butter, more water, same price, and now, apparently, a delightful side of microbial growth. And yet, the truly poignant detail is Mrs. Brain's weary resignation: "It probably won’t stop me buying Pams because it’s the cheaper option but I’ll keep an eye on it." This, dear reader, is the true state of affairs. The consumer, having been thoroughly let down, will return, not out of loyalty, but out of economic necessity. It's a perfectly fucked system, really.
